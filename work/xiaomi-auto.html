A thesis I kept testing against

Second home, not just a smarter car

Xiaomi's whole ecosystem used to centre on 米家 (Mijia) — the private home space, arguably China's best-connected smart-home system. The car extends that same thesis into a private vehicle space: not just transport, but a second home. Everything the team built toward — the ecosystem work across hardware and software — was really in service of that one idea.

On the ecosystem side, that shows up as person-car-home integration: hardware innovation across the HyperOS device family, and software that lets one account carry your settings and control seamlessly between car and home.

What that meant for the interface I was designing

  • Information access as personal, not fixed. Almost everything in the cabin's system UI is customisable — reflected in the smart cabin as pin-able cards a driver arranges around their own habits, building their own driving module. That's a real departure from how most European automakers approach the same screen.
  • Designing for "body memory." The system remembers a driver's customisations and comfort modes, so the car feels like an extension of home even when it's parked and you're just sitting in it, not driving.
  • HMI that works backwards from most interaction design. Most product design tries to increase time-on-screen. In-vehicle HMI does the opposite: the goal is to minimise how long a driver's eyes are on the display. That constraint is closer to B2B design thinking than consumer app design — information has to be delivered as fast as possible, and understood instantly and intuitively, because a few extra seconds of attention has real consequences.

Reflection — what I learnt from Xiaomi

01 · Industry understanding — the brilliance of HMI design lies in "what you can't see"

Hardware constraints, cross-scenario consistency, and multi-vehicle compatibility — imperceptible to users — precisely determine whether the experience is good or bad. This is something I only truly understood after working on eight modules: the hardest part of a product is often not on the screen.

02 · AI and design — a reverse-engineering tool, not a drawing tool

I'm used to using Figma Make and Claude to reverse-engineer design decisions, explore multiple possibilities, and simulate interaction logic. AI can't replace design judgment, but it lets one person do the work of three — a real leverage point in a fast-paced car company.

03 · Designer's capabilities — rigour and flexibility, using design to supplement hardware

HMI designers face hardware constraints (screen, seat, viewing distance) and product ambitions at the same time. Apple's Dynamic Island is a prime example of "using design to compensate for hardware" — in the early stages of Chinese automakers, this ability was scarce.

04 · Cross-market design — switching between Chinese speed and European standards

Domestic EV users prefer a "flexible framework" style of functional density (versus Tesla's minimalist full-screen design), while the European market has strict requirements for compliance, readability and viewing-distance standards. This experience made me comfortable switching quickly between the two design languages.
