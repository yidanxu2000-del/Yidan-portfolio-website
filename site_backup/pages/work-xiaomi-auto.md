## Reflection — What I Learnt From Xiaomi

**01 · Industry Understanding — The Brilliance Of HMI Design Lies In "What You Can't See".**
Hardware constraints, cross-scenario consistency, and multi-vehicle compatibility, which are imperceptible to users, precisely determine whether the user experience is good or bad. This is something I only truly understood after working on eight modules — the most difficult part of a product is often not on the screen.

**02 · AI and Design — AI Is A "Design Reverse Engineering Tool," Not A "Drawing Tool."**
I'm used to using Figma Make and Claude to reverse-engineer design decisions, explore multiple possibilities, and simulate interaction logic. AI can't replace design judgment, but it can enable one person to perform the work of three people — which is a real leverage point in a fast-paced car company.

**03 · Designer's Capabilities — Rigorousness And Flexibility Coexist: Using Design To Supplement Hardware**
HMI designers simultaneously face hardware constraints (screen, seat, viewing distance) and product ambitions. Apple's Dynamic Island is a prime example of "using design to compensate for hardware." In the early stages of Chinese automakers, this ability to use design to compensate for and gain space was scarce.

**04 · Cross-market design — Switching Between Chinese Speed And European Standards**
Domestic EV users prefer a "flexible framework" style of functional density (vs. Tesla's minimalist full-screen design), while the European market has strict requirements for compliance, readability, and viewing distance standards. This experience has made me accustomed to quickly switching between the two design languages and understanding the rationale behind each.
