## A Unified Design Language For 5 B2B Apps — And The First Time AI Entered Our Design Process.
As a design intern embedded in Huawei's B2B department, I proposed design improvements grounded in real user behaviour — across three projects covering user identity, structural optimisation, and cross-app consistency.

- 5 — B2B apps in scope
- 4 — Professional roles studied
- 3 — Tasks delivered
- 2X — Data + interview methodology

## Task 01 · Start From The Big Question
In B2B, identity is everything — yet on most platforms, users are reduced to a name and a job title. How do you bring offline trust online without overcomplicating the product?

🤵 Offline — Professional attire · Business cards. Natural ways to signal who you are and establish trust. Identity is conveyed at first glance.

🥷 Online — A name · A small job title. No way to show expertise, authority, or warmth. Online interactions felt impersonal and less credible than meeting in person.

### The Problem — The B2B Service Space Is Structurally Complex.
An app often serves multiple professional roles simultaneously, whose needs vary greatly. Some roles spend most of their day interacting externally, while others require cross-departmental and cross-regional collaboration. My product team and I worked together to analyze user types and typical interaction scenarios, and two key findings emerged:

- Users assume different roles and responsibilities within the system — the way they build trust and the importance of trust in their work vary greatly depending on their role.
- Users from different backgrounds use the platform in different scenarios, so the information they need to display also changes with the scenario.

### The Goal — Bringing Connections Between People Back To The B2B Online.
Most B2B platforms are designed to be straightforward, restrained, and lacking in personalization — efficiency takes precedence over expression. But a good online experience should preserve, rather than strip away, the trust and emotional connections people have offline. Our goal is to bring back the sense of connection and professional recognition to the B2B online space without increasing the complexity of the experience.

- Bring Offline-Level Trust Online — Use AI to generate visual materials so that the credible visual language of each profession can be read at a glance, instead of being buried in metadata.
- Breaking The Limitations Of Paper Business Cards — Users can switch between two modes — company-oriented or personal-oriented — to use flexibly depending on the scenario.

### Methodology
**First Hand Research** — Through product team workshops and user interviews, I identified several key scenarios for identity verification: first meeting, internal collaboration, and client meetings. The design must hold true in all three scenarios.

**Secondary Research** — Our team studied how "trust" is visually established in different professional contexts. An interesting tension emerged: are people's visual associations with a certain character a genuine signal of ability, or a social stereotype? This tension influenced our subsequent design choices.

### Delivery
- 4 identity cards
- 2 qualification levels
- 2 card formats
- 🪪 New ID card
- 👨 AI Avatar System
- 🎬 Scene coverage

We presented to the product team four occupations and 2 different types of cards that are compatible with 5 apps. Users could establish a stronger and more professional first impression online. The digital business cards helped build trust faster during online interactions. Through the feedback, it is a nice way to fill the gap between offline emotional connection and online efficiency.

**What I learned:** In B2B design, "feeling" is not an option — it determines whether a tool is tolerated or trusted. Most B2B platforms underestimate this. This is the core battleground for B2B differentiation in the next decade.

## Task 02 · Structural Optimization Of B2B Web Platforms
In the second phase of my internship, I moved to another platform — a large-scale enterprise-level web system serving users with multiple roles. Unlike the first project, which was driven by visual and emotional factors, this phase focused on structural design: identifying opportunities to simplify information hierarchy and improve user efficiency based on real behavioral data and user interviews.

🔒 For confidentiality reasons, the original product design and data specifications cannot be displayed here.

### Methodology
**Behavioral Data** — click-through rate, dwell time, and bounce rate tracked by the backend.
**User Interviews** — feedback from: what is convenient, what is confusing, and what do they expect?

### The Framework: Four Behavioral Patterns
This evidence-based classification allows for better decision-making that no longer relies on intuition — each suggestion corresponds to a data signal and a behavioral hypothesis.

### Applications: Three Examples
**Clear Signal** — When a feature consistently exhibits low click-through rates, low dwell time, and high bounce rate, it becomes a candidate for repositioning, or even deletion.

**Mixing Signal** — High click rates and short dwell times usually mean accidental clicks or quick recognition that "this isn't what I want." Interviews helped us clarify the reasons, sometimes all that's needed is clearer labeling and positioning.

**Buried Value** — Low click-through rate but long dwell time — the feature is valuable, but it's hard to find. The solution should be to fix visibility and location, not the feature itself.

## Task 03 · Cross-Application Consistency Of 5 B2B Applications
Alongside the previous two projects, I was also responsible for maintaining design consistency across five B2B applications — analyzing the business structure of each application, identifying inconsistencies in shared components, and aligning with the design team to a unified design standard. This kind of work is quiet — it doesn't produce dramatic "before and after" comparisons. But it's the foundation that makes other things possible. A B2B ecosystem only feels coherent to users when all its components share the same language.

## 3 Things I Took From Huawei

**Industry Understandings — Difference Between B2B And B2C Lies In The "Roles".**
Most B2C designers fail when they try to transition to B2B because they continue to use a "personal preference" framework. But B2B users are not "me" — they are "me in a certain role." Before the role is defined, all UI decisions are guesswork.

**AI and Design — AI Is A "Possibility Generator" Need To Use Properly.**
B2B design has long been criticized for being cold and uniform. AI allows me to generate dozens of career identity directions in an hour — what used to take a week... It doesn't replace design judgment, but it reduces the "cost of exploration" to near zero.

**Data vs. Intuition — Subjective And Objective Must Be Cross-Validated.**
High click-through rate + short dwell time ≠ user satisfaction. Behavioral data tells you "what happened," interviews tell you "why." Both are indispensable. This is the most memorable lesson from my internship.
